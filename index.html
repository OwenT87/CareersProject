Construction Manager/Project Manager

What do They do and What are Some Similarities and Differences?

These managers do a wide variety of tasks. They are the head of the hydra. A construction manager and a project manager have many responsibilities in common. However, a project manager oversees a range of specific projects, which can include construction, engineering, technology, and more, while a construction manager focuses solely on construction projects. Both roles require ensuring that projects are completed on time, within budget, and according to all requirements. They are also responsible for planning for risks, quality control, and similar tasks. Overall, these two professions are very similar; the main difference is that a project manager can work in various industries, whereas a construction manager is limited to construction. There are even times when these two jobs overlap.

What Kind of Eductaional Requirements or Degrees They Need?

To become a project manager or construction manager, the requirements are very similar, as you might expect. You need some education, important job-related skills, and relevant experience. There are several different degrees you could pursue. For construction management, the most straightforward path is a degree in construction management. However, if you want to be a project manager, there are a variety of degrees you can earn, mostly related to business, technology, or engineering. Generally, it does not matter if you have a bachelor’s or master’s degree, but some opportunities may require a master’s. These positions also require experience, which is typically gained by working as an assistant project manager or assistant construction manager.

Work Conditions, Income, and Benefits

These two proffesions do a lot of on site work, but they also do a lot in the office working things out with everyone else that is apart of the project. Both professions have an average income of $100,000 per year, and they also get paid time off, health insurance, retierment plans, bonus, and more.

What is the job outlook?

Once you earn your degree, your career progression largely depends on your situation and how hard you work. Typically, you would start as an assistant project manager or assistant construction manager, then advance to project manager or construction manager. After that, you could be promoted to senior project manager or senior construction manager. Eventually, you might become president, CEO, or vice president. Ultimately, your advancement depends on your circumstances and dedication.

Electrician

Does an Electrician do?

An electrician has to do quite a bit. They have to install, maintain, and repair electrical power, communications, lighting, and control systems. This can involve a number a different skills mostly with wires. However an electrician may also need to know a lot of buisness skills. However there are two different types of electricians: residential and commercial as stated by intercoast.edu.

Residential electricians work in homes and small buildings. They are responsible for installing and maintaining the electrical systems in these buildings.

Commercial electricians work in larger buildings and facilities. They are responsible for installing and maintaining the electrical systems in these buildings.

They will do a lot of the same things just on siginificanlty different scale. Commercial electricians will do things on a much larger scale involving higher voltage, more regulations, larger and more equipment, and a few other things.

What Programs and Training is Required?

There are a few steps to becoming an electrician. First, you need a high school diploma. Next, you need to get a Journeyman's license. To qualify for a Journeyman's license, you often need 8,000 hours of electrical trade work experience plus 576 hours of classroom training. After meeting all the requirements, you can apply to take the Journeyman's exam. Once you pass, you can move on to get a Master Electrician Certification, which requires at least 2 years as a Journeyman and 12,000 hours of electrical work experience in the past 6 years. After that, you can apply for the Master Electrician exam. Once you pass, you can either remain a Master Electrician or get a contractor's license, which allows you to operate independently or start your own business. To do this, you need to pass another exam for the Contractor's License. From my understanding, that is the general process.

Work Conditions, Income, and Benefits

Based on Indeed.com, the average salary for an electrician is $30.33 per hour, with $9,438 in overtime pay, thats quite a bit of green. There are many benefits that come with being an electrician, the biggest being job security. Also the pay might not start out high, but as you progress in your career you can earn a substantial income. Additionally, if you are in a union, you can receive benefits such as health insurance, retirement plans, and paid time off.

An electrician may have to work in a variety of different situations. These can range from working inside a warm house to being outdoors in cold, snowy conditions. Electricians may also be exposed to dangers such as electrical shocks and fires. Additionally, they might have to work overtime in important situations, but this can provide opportunities to earn more money.

What is the Job Outlook?

As an electrician, you are always improving and moving up. At the start, you progress to a Journeyman, then to a Master Electrician. Once you become a Master Electrician, you have the option to get a Contractor's License, which allows you to work for yourself. This alone involves a lot of progression. However, if you stay a Master Electrician, you will continue to progress, start earning more money, and take on more roles. There are also a number of different types of electricians you can become, which adds to your abilities and résumé.

Pros, Cons, and My Opinion

Pros

  • The pay is solid, and you don't have to pay for a degree.
  • It is an easier way to start a business—not easy, but easier and possibly simpler.
  • As mentioned before, job security is a great pro; you don't have to worry about losing your job due to demand.

Cons

  • The working conditions for electricians are not always pleasant.
  • You may have to work overtime, even when it is inconvenient.
  • It can be a dangerous job, so you have to be careful. The currents could shock you and your brain.

My Opinion

After learning about becoming an electrician, I genuinely think it could be a good option. I like how straightforward the progression is, and it seems relatively simple to become your own boss and start your own business. I know it won't be easy by any means, but from what I've learned, it does seem more feasible. I also like the idea of learning a lot of different things, and I appreciate the possibility of making a decent amount of money straight out of high school.

Military

Branches of the Military and What They Do

There are six different branches of the military. Each branches role isn't a pittance in fact, each one plays a crucial role in our government . Each branch also specializes in different aspects and areas that work together to keep everyone safe.

  • The Army - "The Army is the largest branch of the nation and takes care of land-based operations and combat" (Army.mil).
  • The Navy - "The Navy protects America's goals and tasks at sea while also conducting maritime operations" (Navy.com).
  • Marine Corps - "The Marine Corps is a force designed to be ready for quick deployment and operations from the sea" (Marines.mil).
  • The Air Force - "The Air Force covers air and space operations" (Airforce.com).
  • The Coast Guard - "The Coast Guard works with the Navy on maritime operations while also being responsible for law enforcement and search-and-rescue operations" (uscg.mil).
  • The Space Force - "The Space Force focuses on protecting America's goals in space, such as surveillance, communication, and navigation" (spaceforce.mil).

Each of the branches has three parts: active duty, reserve components, and some sort of supporting group. For the samke of simplicity, we will discuss this in the context of the Army, but every branch has its own version. Active duty is what most people typically think of when they talk about the Army. It consists of soldiers and all the extra jobs you can do in the Army. I like to think of it as using the Army as your career. On the other hand, the reserve components are people who can still get deployed and go through some training, but it is more of a side gig while they maintain their civilian jobs. Then there is the supporting group. In each branch, they operate a little differently and may not always be considered their own thing, but they are still there. In the Army, they are called the Institutional Army. This group helps with training, supplies, and the overall preparation of the Army.

Jobs in the Military

In reference to active duty military, there are mountains of jobs. There are a few different categories of jobs that you can work in, however. The most common and most consistent throughout the branches are the following: combat, support, specialized, administrative roles, and miscellaneous roles. Now, depending on which branch you look at, the jobs are different. For example, infantry—a combat role—is in the Army and the Marine Corps, but not in the others. If we look at engineering—a support role—it is in every branch; however, the engineers are different depending on the branch. Furthermore, an engineer in the Army might work on tanks or things of that nature, while an engineer in the Air Force might work on planes. Overall, there are numerous categories and types of jobs, and they all go parallel with their respective branch. Today, I want to talk about the special forces. Only four of the branches have special forces, but there are also a few different types of special forces in each branch.

  • The Army - Green Berets, 75th Ranger Regiment, Delta Forces, and Night Stalkers
  • The Navy - Navy Seals, DEVGRU, and Navy SWCCs
  • The Marine Corps - MARSOC, and Marine Raiders
  • The Air Force - AFSOC, Special Tactics, CCT, and Pararescumen(PJ)
